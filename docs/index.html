Understanding the Complaint Gap

"Why do some areas remain dark, yet residents never complain?"

Traditional maintenance relies on 311 complaints. But some communities face barriers to reporting: language, time, distrust, or lack of awareness. Satellite data reveals what complaints miss.

This study uses VIIRS satellite data to objectively identify dark areas, bypassing the complaint system to find underserved communities directly.

Major Findings

62 Underserved Areas Where Darkness, Low Complaints, and Low Income Converge

The Paradox: The darkest areas file 76% fewer complaints than city average

The Pattern: These are also the lowest-income neighborhoods ($42,800 vs. $67,200 average)

The Implication: The 311 system systematically misses the communities that need help most

Finding 2: Citywide Decline

Philadelphia lighting is declining citywide.

Model predicts 582 block groups will experience continued brightness decline

Current status: 80% of Philadelphia block groups showed decreasing light levels from 2022-2024

Priority intervention needed before infrastructure failure.

Critical Context

Philadelphia's streetlights date back to the 1970s. Our 2022-2024 data captures the critical period before the $91M LED upgrade project (launched August 2023)‚Äîdocumenting peak infrastructure decline.

This analysis establishes the baseline for proactive lighting equity monitoring.

Every streetlight outage represents more than a technical failure‚Äîit reflects a gap in the urban social contract.

When repairs take weeks in some neighborhoods and days in others, we're witnessing the compounding of disadvantage. Low-income communities already face higher crime rates, limited transit options, and fewer private vehicles. Prolonged darkness adds another layer of vulnerability.

Each light restored is not just an infrastructure fix‚Äîit represents restored public trust.

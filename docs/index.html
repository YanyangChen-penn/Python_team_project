Unequal Nights

Mapping Lighting Equity, Safety, and Urban Mobility in Philadelphia

Author

Zicheng Xiang, Zhe Fang, Yanyang Chen

Published

November 7, 2025

From Darkness to Decision

“Data should not only reveal darkness, but it should also help cities decide where to shine next.”

Philadelphia’s nights are unevenly illuminated. Downtown shines brightly, while parts of North Philadelphia and Kensington remain in darkness for weeks after lights fail. This pattern reflects spatiotemporal inequality in infrastructure maintenance—where some neighborhoods get rapid repairs and others wait in the dark.

Urban Problem and Motivation

The consequences of unequal lighting are threefold:

Three Critical Impacts
  1. Objective Risk: Concentration of street crimes and traffic accidents
  2. Subjective Fear: Diminished sense of safety and comfort
  3. Social Inequity: Low-income and minority areas face higher exposure to lighting poverty

Research Question

Core Question

In what ways does urban lighting influence perceived and actual safety among people navigating Philadelphia at night?

Research Goals

Our project pursues four interconnected objectives:

Goal 1: Quantify Lighting Equity

Measure streetlight outage density, repair delay, and socioeconomic correlates through our Lighting Equity Index (LEI).

Goal 2: Reveal Light-Safety Mechanisms

Test links between lighting quality, crime rates, and perceived insecurity using spatial regression models.

Goal 3: Integrate Mobility Exposure

Evaluate how night transit (SEPTA) and Indego bike networks intersect with lighting coverage via our Night Transit Exposure Index (NTE).

Goal 4: Predict and Prioritize

Use machine learning (Random Forest) to forecast future ‘dark spots’ and propose repair corridors.

Why This Matters

Every streetlight outage represents more than a technical failure—it reflects a gap in the urban social contract. When repairs take weeks in some neighborhoods and days in others, we’re not just seeing infrastructure inequality; we’re witnessing the compounding of disadvantage.

The Equity Imperative

Low-income communities already face higher crime rates, limited transit options, and fewer private vehicles. Prolonged darkness adds another layer of vulnerability, constraining mobility, economic opportunity, and quality of life.

By transforming 311 maintenance data into actionable insight, we demonstrate how spatial data science can illuminate pathways to justice—one streetlight at a time.

# Methodology Overview

Our workflow follows the complete data-science pipeline:

::: {.panel-tabset}

## Phase 1: Diagnostics
- Aggregate outages by street segment per quarter
- Compute **Outage Density (OD)** and **Repair Delay (RD)**
- Generate Darkness Evolution Maps

## Phase 2: Equity Analysis
- Standardize OD, RD, poverty, and vehicle ownership
- Derive integrated **Lighting Equity Index (LEI)** via PCA
- Identify lighting poverty hotspots

## Phase 3: Spatial Statistics
- Moran's I and LISA for clustering detection
- Spatial Lag and Error Models
- Quantify crime ~ lighting relationship

## Phase 4: Perception Gap
- Map objective crime vs subjective fear
- Analyze 311 complaint patterns
- Identify perception-reality mismatches

## Phase 5: Mobility Integration
- Compute nighttime SEPTA headways
- Build 10-min walk buffers via OSMnx
- Calculate **Night Transit Exposure Index (NTE)**

## Phase 6: Prediction
- Random Forest on OD/RD, road density, zoning, season
- Generate dark-zone risk probability maps
- Rank priority corridors

## Phase 7: Visualization
- Interactive Panel Dashboard
- Temporal slider and narrative storytelling
- Theme: "From Darkness to Decision"

:::
